--- name: att_attention_orchestrator description: "Build project attention through positioning, proof, launch, content, and distribution." user-invocable: true disable-model-invocation: false metadata: {"openclaw":{"emoji":"\ud83d\udce3"}} --- # Attention Orchestrator ## Purpose - Build project attention through positioning, proof, launch, content, and distribution. - This is the meta-skill for attention work: it diagnoses the current attention surface, identifies the highest-leverage plays, and routes into the right specialist skills for execution. Think of it as the conductor, not the instrument. ## Use when - Use when the user wants more awareness, traction, interest, buzz, demand, or narrative strength around a project. - Use when attention work feels scattered — lots of activity, no coherent strategy. - Use when planning a launch, building an audience, or restarting stalled growth. ## Avoid when - Do not use when the project has no real audience, no clear problem, or no credible proof — attention work on a hollow product just reveals the hollowness faster. - If the ask is for a single asset (a thread, a landing page, a press angle), route directly to the specialist skill instead. - Do not use as a substitute for product work — attention amplifies what exists, it doesn't fix what's missing. ## Inputs to gather - Current attention surface: what do people already know? What's the existing narrative? Where does the project show up today? - Audience reality: who are they, where do they gather, what do they already believe about this space? - Proof inventory: what results, testimonials, data, or demonstrations can you point to? What's still missing? - Team capacity: how many people, how much time, what channels can they actually sustain? - The specific ask: launch, ongoing demand, activation, conversion, or reputation building? ## Operating rules - **Attention is created by message-market fit plus distribution, not volume alone.** Shouting into the void doesn't create attention — a sharp message delivered to the right audience does. Get the wedge right before scaling distribution. - **Lead with a real wedge, real proof, and a clear next step.** A wedge is the specific tension or insight that makes someone stop scrolling. Proof is what makes them stay. A next step is what makes them act. All three are required. - **Choose a small number of channels that fit the audience and the team.** Spreading across 6 channels with 1 person guarantees thin, forgettable presence everywhere. Depth on 2 channels beats spray on 10. - **Sequence: proof → narrative → distribution.** Don't build distribution before you have something worth distributing. Don't polish narrative before you have proof to anchor it. ## OpenClaw tool pattern - Ground messaging in real proof, user language, and the current product truth before polishing copy. - Read existing site copy, launch assets, notes, or research so the new output fits the system instead of floating beside it. - When external facts or competitors matter, verify them before using them in public-facing content. ## Expanded workflow 1. **Audit the current attention surface.** Map: audience awareness (who knows about you?), narrative strength (what do they believe?), proof quality (what can you prove?), channel presence (where do you show up?), and asset inventory (what content exists?). Score each: strong / adequate / weak / missing. 2. **Identify the binding constraint.** Is it proof (nothing to believe)? Narrative (nothing compelling to say)? Distribution (nobody to hear it)? Or capacity (no time to execute)? Fix the weakest link first. 3. **Choose the highest-leverage plays.** Based on the constraint: weak proof → `att_proof_mining`; weak narrative → `att_positioning_workshop` or `att_message_house`; weak distribution → `att_content_calendar` or `att_zero_audience_distribution`; weak assets → specialist skill for the needed format. 4. **Sequence the execution.** Order the specialist skills so each builds on the last. Example: proof mining → message house → launch plan → content calendar. 5. **Return a coherent attention plan.** Compile: current surface diagnosis, binding constraint, priority plays, execution sequence, and the first action to take. ## Output contract - Attention surface audit: scores for awareness, narrative, proof, distribution, assets - Binding constraint identification: what's weakest and why - Priority plays: which specialist skills to use, in what order - Execution sequence: what happens first, second, third - First action: the smallest concrete step to start ## Failure modes to avoid - **Orchestrating without proof.** Launching attention work without credible proof creates visibility for claims that can't survive scrutiny. Build proof first. - **Spreading too thin.** Activating 6 channels with 1 person guarantees burnout and thin presence. Depth beats breadth until you have capacity to expand. - **Treating attention as the goal.** Attention is a means to an outcome: activation, conversion, or reputation. Define the outcome before designing the attention plan. - **Skipping diagnosis.** If you jump straight to execution without auditing the current surface, you'll optimize the wrong thing. ## Handoff cues - State the diagnosis: which part of the attention surface is weakest. - Name the specialist skills in execution order. - Flag the first action and who should own it. - Note what proof is still missing and needs to be gathered before attention work can proceed. ## Example invocation - Slash: `/att_attention_orchestrator <task>` - Natural language: "Use Attention Orchestrator to diagnose what's missing and build a coherent attention plan." - Example: "I'm doing a lot of marketing but nothing is clicking — help me find the real bottleneck." - Example: "I'm launching next month — help me sequence the attention work." - Often paired with: `att_message_house`, `att_launch_plan`, `att_proof_mining`, `att_positioning_workshop` ## Quality bar - A good attention plan identifies the binding constraint and sequences the fix — it doesn't just list activities. - The plan must be executable with the team's actual capacity, not an aspirational blueprint that requires 5 more people. - If the diagnosis doesn't change where you'd spend time tomorrow, it's not specific enough. ## Related workflows - **Launch sequence:** `att_attention_orchestrator` → `att_proof_mining` → `att_message_house` → `att_launch_plan` → `att_content_calendar` - **Audience from zero:** `att_attention_orchestrator` → `att_zero_audience_distribution` → `att_community_playbook` → `att_content_calendar` - **Narrative sharpening:** `att_positioning_workshop` → `att_message_house` → `att_proof_mining` → `att_thread_writer` / `att_short_video_script`
