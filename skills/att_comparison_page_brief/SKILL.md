--- name: att_comparison_page_brief description: "Design comparison pages that win trust with fair framing, proof, and a clear reason to choose." user-invocable: true disable-model-invocation: true metadata: {"openclaw":{"emoji":"\u2696\ufe0f"}} --- # Comparison Page Brief ## Purpose - Design comparison pages that win trust with fair framing, proof, and a clear reason to choose. - Comparison pages are high-intent pages: the buyer already knows they need a solution and is deciding between options. This skill designs a page that wins the comparison by being more useful, more honest, and more specific than the competitor's version. ## Use when - Use when prospects are actively comparing your project to alternatives, workarounds, or incumbents. - Use when search traffic includes "[your product] vs [competitor]" queries. - Use when sales reports that "we keep losing to [competitor] on [specific feature]." ## Avoid when - Do not write attack pages that overclaim or misrepresent competitors — they backfire with savvy buyers. - If you don't have real differentiators, a comparison page will highlight that gap. Fix the product first. - Do not use for SEO-gaming pages with no real comparison content. ## Inputs to gather - Target alternatives: which competitors, which workarounds, which "do nothing" scenarios. - Buyer evaluation criteria: what dimensions do they actually compare on? (Not what you wish they compared on.) - Your real advantages: where do you genuinely win? Where do you genuinely lose? - Available proof: benchmarks, customer results, feature audits, pricing comparisons. - Competitive claims: what do competitors say about themselves? What do their users say? ## Operating rules - **Fair comparisons build trust better than chest-thumping.** Acknowledge where competitors are strong. Buyers who see honest tradeoffs trust your wins more. A page that says "we win on everything" reads like marketing, not analysis. - **Focus on fit, tradeoffs, and specific differences.** "Better" is meaningless. "Faster setup (5 min vs 2 hours), but fewer integrations" is a tradeoff the buyer can evaluate. Name the tradeoffs honestly. - **Back claims with evidence.** Every advantage you claim should link to: a benchmark, a customer result, a feature demo, or a specific capability proof. Unsupported claims are ignored by sophisticated buyers. - **Design for the skimmer and the deep-diver.** Lead with a summary verdict table. Follow with detailed sections. The buyer who's 90% decided needs the table. The buyer who's skeptical needs the details. - **Include the "do nothing" and "spreadsheet" alternatives.** Many buyers aren't choosing between you and a competitor — they're choosing between you and staying with their current manual process. Name it and show why it's worse. ## OpenClaw tool pattern - Verify competitor claims against their actual product or public reviews — don't rely on outdated assumptions. - Read existing landing pages, sales decks, and objection maps to ensure the comparison page aligns with the rest of the proof system. - Use `res_competitor_scan` data when available for factual grounding. ## Expanded workflow 1. **Identify the comparison target and buyer intent.** What alternatives are they comparing? What stage of the buying process are they in? What decision are they trying to make? 2. **Map the buyer's real evaluation criteria.** Not your feature list — their decision dimensions. Common: time to value, total cost, capability breadth, ease of use, support quality, integration depth, compliance. 3. **Honest feature/capability comparison.** For each criterion: where do you win? Where are you tied? Where do you lose? Mark each honestly. The losses make the wins credible. 4. **Design the page structure.** Summary verdict table → Key differentiators with proof → Honest tradeoffs section → Customer proof (case studies, quotes) → CTA. 5. **Write the proof plan.** For each claimed advantage: what evidence links to it? Benchmark? Customer story? Feature demo? If no proof exists, flag it as a gap — don't make the claim. 6. **Review for fairness and credibility.** Would a neutral third party agree with this comparison? Would a customer of the competitor feel fairly represented? ## Output contract - Comparison targets with buyer intent - Evaluation criteria mapped to real buyer decisions - Feature comparison: wins, ties, losses per criterion - Page structure: verdict table → differentiators → tradeoffs → proof → CTA - Proof plan: evidence linked to each claimed advantage - Known gaps: claims that still lack proof ## Failure modes to avoid - **Winning on invented criteria.** If you define the comparison dimensions to only show your strengths, buyers will notice and distrust the whole page. Use the criteria they actually care about. - **Ignoring where you lose.** A page with no acknowledged weaknesses reads as propaganda. Named tradeoffs build credibility. - **Stale competitor data.** Competitor features and pricing change. If your comparison data is 6 months old, it's probably wrong. - **No "do nothing" alternative.** The biggest competitor is often the status quo. Name it and show the cost of inaction. ## Handoff cues - State the comparison targets and which criteria are most favorable/unfavorable. - Name the proof gaps — claims that need evidence before the page can go live. - Flag any competitor claims that need verification against the current product. ## Example invocation - Slash: `/att_comparison_page_brief <task>` - Example: "Prospects keep asking how we compare to [Competitor X] — design a fair comparison page." - Often paired with: `res_competitor_scan`, `att_message_house`, `att_proof_mining`, `sales_objection_map` ## Quality bar - A strong comparison brief makes the page useful even to a skeptical buyer. - Every claimed advantage must link to specific evidence. No proof = no claim. - At least one acknowledged tradeoff must be visible — pages that claim to win everywhere are not credible. ## Related workflows - **Competitive positioning:** `res_competitor_scan` → `att_positioning_workshop` → `att_comparison_page_brief` → `att_landing_page_story` - **Sales enablement:** `sales_objection_map` → `att_comparison_page_brief` (address top objections) → `att_faq_objection_bank` - **SEO capture:** keyword research → `att_comparison_page_brief` → `att_seo_page_brief` → publish
