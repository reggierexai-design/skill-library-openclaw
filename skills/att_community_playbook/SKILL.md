--- name: att_community_playbook description: "Design community and audience-building loops that create repeated interaction around the project." user-invocable: true disable-model-invocation: true metadata: {"openclaw":{"emoji":"\ud83d\udc65"}} --- # Community Playbook ## Purpose - Design community and audience-building loops that create repeated interaction around the project. - Communities don't grow from broadcasting — they grow from repeated value exchange and identity. This skill designs the loops that make people show up, participate, and stay: contribution loops, recognition loops, and value loops that compound over time. ## Use when - Use when the team wants durable attention from a niche audience, not just one-off launch spikes. - Use when moving from "audience" (broadcast) to "community" (two-way interaction). - Use when a Discord, Slack, forum, or user group needs a growth and engagement strategy. ## Avoid when - Do not use when there is no clear community surface, niche, or reason for people to return — a community without shared purpose is a ghost town. - If you don't have even 10 early users who care, build those relationships 1:1 first. Community infrastructure without community is premature. - If the ask is just "set up a Discord," use `comm_discord_setup_nurture` instead. ## Inputs to gather - Current community surface: does anything exist? How many active members? What's the current energy level? - The shared purpose: why would these people gather? What do they have in common beyond the product? - Contribution capacity: what can members create, share, or help with? - Team capacity: how much time can the team invest in community management? - Success signals: what does a healthy community look like for this project? (Not vanity metrics — real engagement indicators.) ## Operating rules - **Community grows around repeated value and identity, not posting volume.** People return because they get something they can't get elsewhere and because they feel known. Design the value exchange, not the content calendar. - **Choose one primary gathering surface before expanding.** Discord OR Slack OR a forum — not all three. Fragmentation kills momentum. Go where the audience already is, then expand if the first surface thrives. - **Give people a reason to participate, not only watch.** Lurkers become members when they have something to contribute. Design contribution on-ramps: questions to answer, templates to share, showcases to post, feedback to give. - **Design the recognition loop.** People stay where they feel seen. Showcase top contributors. Highlight member wins. Give roles or status for consistent participation. Recognition is the fuel of community. - **Start small, stay consistent.** 10 engaged members beat 1000 idle followers. A weekly ritual (AMA, showcase, challenge) builds habit. Consistency beats intensity. ## OpenClaw tool pattern - Read existing community platforms, member lists, and engagement data before proposing strategy. - Ground the playbook in real member behavior: what do they already do? What do they ask for? What gets them excited? - When referencing competitor communities, verify their activity levels — some look active but are mostly spam. ## Expanded workflow 1. **Define the audience and community promise.** Who gathers here? What's the shared purpose? What does a member get that they can't get elsewhere? The promise must be specific: "A place for indie hackers building AI tools to share what's working" beats "A community for AI enthusiasts." 2. **Choose the primary channel.** Where does the audience already spend time? Start there. Don't make them come to you — go to them first, then build your own surface when you have critical mass. 3. **Design the value loop.** What brings people back? Weekly content? Peer help? Exclusive access? Early previews? Design the recurring value exchange. 4. **Design the contribution loop.** What can members create or share? Questions, templates, showcases, feedback, introductions. Lower the barrier: the first contribution should take < 2 minutes. 5. **Design the recognition loop.** How do you spotlight members? Featured projects, member spotlights, contributor roles, leaderboards (use carefully — they can create toxic competition). 6. **Set the cadence.** What happens daily, weekly, monthly? Daily: member questions and help. Weekly: a structured event or content drop. Monthly: a showcase or milestone. Write the ritual calendar. 7. **Plan the growth path.** How does the community get from 10 to 100 to 1000? Stage 1: founder-led, high-touch. Stage 2: member-led content, founder moderation. Stage 3: self-sustaining, founder as participant. 8. **Return the playbook.** Compile: promise, channel, loops, cadence, growth stages, and the first week's actions. ## Output contract - Community promise: who gathers, why, what they get - Primary channel with rationale - Value loop: what brings people back - Contribution loop: what members create/share - Recognition loop: how members get seen - Ritual calendar: daily, weekly, monthly cadence - Growth stages: from 10 to 100 to 1000 - First week actions: the smallest concrete steps ## Failure modes to avoid - **Broadcasting into a void.** Creating a Discord, posting announcements, and waiting for members. Communities need seeding, nurturing, and early 1:1 relationship building. - **Fragmenting across platforms.** Discord + Slack + forum + subreddit = 4 ghost towns. Go deep on one first. - **Vanity metrics over engagement.** 10,000 members with 5 active is worse than 50 members with 30 active. Measure engagement rate, not headcount. - **Founder burnout.** If the community can't function without the founder present every day, it's not a community — it's a following. Design for member-led activity early. - **No contribution on-ramp.** If the only way to participate is to post original content, most people will lurk forever. Design easy first contributions. ## Handoff cues - State the community promise and primary channel — another operator needs to know where to show up. - Name the first week's actions and who owns each. - Flag what's still undefined: the weekly ritual, the recognition system, or the growth trigger. - Note the stage: pre-launch, seeded, or active — the next actions differ by stage. ## Example invocation - Slash: `/att_community_playbook <task>` - Natural language: "Use Community Playbook to design the loops that make people show up, participate, and stay." - Example: "I have 20 beta users — help me build a community around them instead of losing them after launch." - Often paired with: `att_content_calendar`, `comm_discord_setup_nurture`, `att_proof_mining`, `att_founder_story` ## Quality bar - A good community plan compounds relationships, not just impressions. - The contribution loop must have a < 2-minute first action. If the easiest way to participate takes 10 minutes, most people won't. - The growth stages must be realistic for the team's actual capacity. - The playbook should survive the founder taking a week off. ## Related workflows - **Community from zero:** `att_community_playbook` → `comm_discord_setup_nurture` (set up platform) → `comm_beta_group_management` (seed with beta users) → `att_content_calendar` (sustain with content) - **Launch community:** `att_launch_plan` → `att_community_playbook` (capture launch interest) → `comm_discord_setup_nurture` → `comm_user_advocacy_cultivate` (find champions) - **Retention through community:** `prod_retention_loop` → `att_community_playbook` → `comm_user_advocacy_cultivate`
