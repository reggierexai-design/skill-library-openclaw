# Case Study Builder ## Purpose - Shape a customer story into a credible case study with before, after, mechanism, and proof. - A case study is the most powerful proof asset because it's specific, verifiable, and relatable. This skill turns raw customer outcomes into a structured narrative that a skeptical buyer can trust and a sales team can deploy. ## Use when - Use when the project has a user win, transformation, or measurable result worth publishing. - Use when sales needs proof assets for objection handling or pipeline acceleration. - Use when the landing page needs credibility beyond claims. ## Avoid when - Do not use when there is no real result or no permission to tell the story — a case study without consent or evidence is a liability. - If the outcome isn't measurable or verifiable, it's a testimonial, not a case study. Use `att_social_proof_pack` instead. - Do not ghostwrite fictional case studies — the story must come from real customer data and quotes. ## Inputs to gather - Customer name, role, and context: who are they, what were they doing before, what's their world like? - The before state: what problem existed, what was it costing, what had they already tried? - The change: what did they adopt, how did they implement, what was the mechanism? - The after state: what improved, by how much, over what timeframe? - Customer quotes: raw language from the customer, not marketing-reworded versions. - Permission status: can we use their name? Company? Data? - Target audience: who's reading this case study and what do they need to believe? ## Operating rules - **A case study needs context, conflict, solution, and proof.** Context: who is this customer and why should the reader care? Conflict: what was going wrong? Solution: what did they do and how does it work? Proof: what changed, measured how? Missing any element, the case study collapses into either a testimonial (no mechanism) or a feature demo (no customer). - **Specificity beats adjectives.** "Saved 40 hours per month" beats "dramatically improved efficiency." "Went from 3-day deploy cycles to same-day" beats "streamlined operations." Every claim needs a number, a timeframe, or a before/after comparison. - **Keep the customer as the protagonist.** The customer did the work. The product was the tool. Case studies that make the product the hero read like ads; case studies that make the customer the hero read like stories. - **Show the mechanism, not just the outcome.** "Revenue went up 30%" is a claim. "They automated the manual lead scoring that was taking 10 hours/week, which let the team focus on closing instead of qualifying, which increased close rate from 12% to 18%" is a mechanism. The mechanism is what makes the outcome believable and repeatable. - **Include the warts.** What was hard about adoption? What didn't work immediately? Perfect case studies are suspicious. Credibility comes from honest friction, not from erasing it. ## OpenClaw tool pattern - Ground the narrative in real customer data and quotes, not marketing rewrites. - Read existing case studies, landing pages, and sales decks to ensure the new case study fits the existing proof system. - When extracting metrics, verify them against available data — don't round up or embellish. ## Expanded workflow 1. **Inventory the customer story and evidence.** Collect: before state metrics, the change event, after state metrics, customer quotes, implementation details, and any friction points. Flag what's verified vs. claimed vs. missing. 2. **Structure the narrative arc.** Framework: Context (who + situation) → Conflict (the pain, with numbers) → Change (what they did, how the product fit) → Result (measured outcome, with timeframe) → Mechanism (why it worked, what's repeatable) → Quote (customer in their own words). 3. **Write the case study.** Lead with the most compelling result. Tell the before vividly enough that the reader recognizes their own pain. Explain the mechanism clearly enough that the reader can imagine it working for them. End with a quote that feels human, not corporate. 4. **Add specificity upgrades.** Replace every vague claim with a number, comparison, or timeframe. "Improved" → "Cut from 5 days to 8 hours." "Significant" → "3x increase." If you can't find the number, flag it as a gap. 5. **Create derivatives.** Short version for landing page (2-3 sentences). Quote pull for social. One-liner for sales decks. Metric line for comparison pages. 6. **Review for credibility.** Would a skeptical buyer believe this? Is every claim backed by a specific metric? Is the customer quote authentic? Is the mechanism clear enough to be repeatable? ## Output contract - Full case study: Context → Conflict → Change → Result → Mechanism → Quote - Specificity audit: every claim upgraded to a number, comparison, or timeframe - Derivatives: landing page version, social quote, sales deck one-liner, metric line - Permission and verification notes: what's confirmed, what's still pending - Credibility gaps: claims that still lack specific evidence ## Failure modes to avoid - **Vague outcomes.** "Improved efficiency" is not a case study result — it's a marketing claim. If the metric isn't specific, the case study isn't done. - **Making the product the hero.** The customer did the work. The product was the tool. Swap the subject and the story gains trust. - **Erasing the friction.** Perfect adoption stories are suspicious. Including what was hard makes the success believable. - **Marketing-rewording quotes.** Customer language is valuable because it's raw. "We were drowning in manual processes" is better than "They leveraged our automation platform to optimize operational workflows." - **Missing the mechanism.** Without the mechanism (HOW the result happened), the case study is a correlation, not a proof. ## Handoff cues - State the case study status: draft, reviewed, customer-approved, published. - Name the derivatives that have been created and where they're deployed. - Flag any metrics that need customer verification before publication. - Note where the case study fits in the proof system: sales deck, landing page, comparison page, email sequences. ## Example invocation - Slash: `/att_case_study_builder <task>` - Natural language: "Use Case Study Builder to turn this customer win into a credible proof asset." - Example: "We have a customer who went from 2-week sprints to daily deploys — build the case study." - Example: "Sales needs a case study for the healthcare vertical — shape this customer story." - Often paired with: `att_proof_mining`, `att_social_proof_pack`, `att_landing_page_story`, `sales_demo_flow` ## Quality bar - A credible case study sells by explaining, not boasting. A skeptical buyer should finish it thinking "I can see how this would work for me." - Every outcome claim must have a specific metric or timeframe. Vague claims are failures. - The mechanism must be clear enough that another customer could replicate the approach. - The customer quote must sound like a human said it, not a PR department. ## Related workflows - **Proof system build:** `att_proof_mining` (extract metrics) → `att_case_study_builder` (structure story) → `att_social_proof_pack` (package for channels) → `att_landing_page_story` (deploy on site) - **Sales enablement:** `att_case_study_builder` → `sales_demo_flow` (integrate into demo) → `sales_objection_map` (match to objections) → `att_comparison_page_brief` (add to comparison) - **Launch proof:** `att_case_study_builder` → `att_launch_plan` (add to launch assets) → `att_email_nurture_sequences` (add to nurture)
